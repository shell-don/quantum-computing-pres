What is Quantum Computing?

Quantum computing is a new technology on the horizon. It’s a new type of computer that exploits quantum principles and mechanisms to achieve super fast calculations. This breakthrough has major implications in the IT world, in particular in cryptography and digital security.

Our issue: The advent of quantum computing, and his implications in cryptography.

Conventional vs. Quantum

All the things that a computer does, it achieves it using a really simple thing, a bit. In theory, a bit is a number that can take two values, either 1 or 0. By combining multiple bits you obtain binary code. In reality, a bit is an electrical impulse.

Quantum computers replace this electrical impulse with other atomic phenomenons with different behaviors, and different possibilities. It’s not a computer under steroïds that can do anything faster, but a new specialized type of computer with different capabilities. Most of it is super fast mathematical computing.

Why Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) matters.

The Collapse of Current Encryption

Suppose you have a stable quantum computer. Then some complex mathematical problems which are the cornerstone of the actual encryption methods for data security, become solvable. Many of the actual security protocols would be breakable in a reasonable amount of time, exposing a lot of sensitive data.

According to NIST - the US agency for technology standards in the industry, it’s possible that such a computer could exist by 2030, at the cost of 1 billion dollars.

Global Supremacy and Data Privacy

Anybody with this kind of computer could easily have access to all of your private data, for example read your emails. But the real threat appears if you apply the same logic for businesses and governments. Sensitive information could be exposed to cyberattacks, such as banking credentials or military records.

Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) is at the cornerstone of future technology supremacy. US governments already use it for classified data to maintain their technological advantage. Some countries (France, Canada, UK…) have decided to limit their exportation.

Other Applications

As quantum computing is a research field, it is useful in many cases where massive calculus is necessary. For example, the chemicals industry where molecule simulation is necessary for the discovery of new materials or pharmaceutical drugs. Other examples include vehicle routing, truck loading, lot sizing, or optimizing the flow around aircrafts.

Technical Solutions

Mathematicians must study new cryptography algorithms for securing communication. NIST has already standardized PQC and urges companies to implement their algorithms as soon as possible.

Legal Solutions

Make a law for limiting the use of quantum computing to research fields. However, legal solutions are only limitations and could not really ban other countries from quantum supremacy.

Individual & Open Source

Developing tech-free solutions: creating more complex passwords and avoiding exposing private data. Using self-hosted password managers. Financing open source projects by international organizations like the UN to avoid conflicts of interest.
